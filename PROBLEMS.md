# Problems to Solve

## Category 1: Emotional & Psychological

| # | Problem | Description |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Procrastination | Knowing what to do but being unable to turn knowledge into action; body holding you back from starting work |
| 2 | Fear-based paralysis | Stress hormones (cortisol/adrenaline) getting released when facing tasks, causing a freeze response |
| 3 | Fear of the unknown | Job loss + visa expiration triggering overthinking, inability to decide, and physical anxiety (a recurring pattern for years) |
| 4 | Intolerance of uncertainty | Freezing up under ambiguous conditions, especially in work/career situations |
| 5 | Emotional avoidance pattern | Amygdala firing too easily, prefrontal cortex not trained to override it |
| 6 | Anxiety and intrusive thoughts | Brain randomly connecting to nervous thoughts; inability to stop the spiral |
| 7 | Fear of announcing plans | Not wanting to share goals before achieving them, driven by fear of public failure and vulnerability |
| 8 | Aiming for the minimum | Fear stopping you from reaching for the pinnacle; inconsistent confidence |
| 9 | Background unhappiness / low-grade depression | Persistent "not happy, worried, anxious" mood with positive attitude on the surface |
| 10 | Childhood emotional neglect (CEN) | Discovered as a root cause during a deep session; disconnection from your own needs and feelings |
| 11 | Difficulty with self-care | Not dressing well, not taking care of your body, not eating properly — tied to not feeling "worth it" |

## App Review — March 2026

### What's Genuinely Strong

- **The concept is sharp.** This isn't another generic to-do app. The emotional layer (pre/post emotion check-ins, approach vs avoidance tracking, start assist for high-tension moments) is a real differentiator. Most productivity apps ignore *why* people don't do things. This addresses that head-on.
- **The focus session flow is well-designed.** Emotion check-in → breathing if tense → strategy selection → timer → post-session reflection. That's a thoughtful UX arc.
- **"Eat the frog" and push analytics** give users data about their own avoidance patterns without being preachy. That's hard to get right.
- **The dashboard quick view** is clean — summary cards with expandable detail is the right pattern for a daily driver.

### Concerns

#### 1. Feature Bloat (Biggest Risk)
Tasks, habits, goals, focus timer, frogs, back-of-mind, chores, interests, interest connections, pillars, milestones, mistake log, daily review, morning launch, energy check-in, grounding tools, emotion tracking, push analytics, approach scores, stretch goals, celebrations, XP/gamification, templates...

For a single user building for themselves, it's fine. For other users, the onboarding curve would be steep.

#### 2. Too Many Dashboard Widgets
The deep dive tab has ~12 components across 4 sub-tabs. Most users won't explore past the first tab. Consider whether some of these earn their screen space with real usage.

#### 3. Emotional Features Assume High Engagement
The approach score, emotion trends, and celebration stats are only meaningful if someone consistently does emotion check-ins. If they skip them (which most users will), those sections show empty states indefinitely.

#### 4. No Clear Hierarchy of What Matters
Tasks, goals, habits, frogs, chores — these overlap conceptually. A new user would wonder: "Where do I put 'clean the kitchen'? Is it a task? A chore? A habit?" The mental model needs to be clearer.

#### 5. Deeply Personal Tooling Dressed as a Product
Features like the grounding button, body tension tracking, and avoidance pattern analysis are genuinely therapeutic tools. That's powerful for personal use, but it means the app sits in an awkward middle ground — too complex for casual productivity users, not clinically validated for mental health use.

### Suggested Next Steps

#### Pause on new features entirely.
The roadmap still has Phases C and E, but what exists now is already more than most productivity apps ship.

#### 1. Use it daily for 2-3 weeks
Find out which features you actually open and which you forget exist. Kill the ones you don't use.

#### 2. Polish over expansion
Loading states, error handling, mobile responsiveness, data export — the unglamorous stuff that makes an app feel solid.

#### 3. Nail the core loop
Tasks + focus + daily review should feel bulletproof before building more peripheral features.

> *The foundation is genuinely good. The risk is building so much that the good parts get buried.*
